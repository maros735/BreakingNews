Under Pressure

When the news of a viral outbreak broke in early 2020, no one could have predicted the scale of disruption it would bring. But for healthcare workers across the world, life changed overnight. They became the frontline warriors against an invisible enemy, donning masks and protective gear as armor against an unprecedented global health crisis.

In hospitals and emergency care units, health workers worked long, exhausting shifts, often without enough protective equipment, all while living in constant fear of infection—not just for themselves, but for their families. Their daily lives were marked by new challenges, shifting guidelines, and difficult decisions. Some would lose colleagues; others would face an unrelenting tide of patients, many of whom would not survive.

In this series, we hear from healthcare workers on the front lines. What was the experience like for them? How did they cope with the physical, mental, and emotional toll? And how do they reflect on those challenging days now that the pandemic is (mostly) behind us?.

A Nursing Student on the Frontlines: Learning Amid Chaos

For Sara (26), a nursing student, the pandemic was not just an academic challenge but a crucible that tested her resolve and adaptability. “Working in healthcare during the pandemic was physically and mentally demanding. The uncertainty and constant protocol changes made each day feel different,” she recalls. As a nursing student, her experience was particularly intense, blending the realities of clinical work with the need to quickly adapt to new roles in a high-stress environment.

“The most difficult part was seeing patients, especially the most vulnerable, isolated and without their families,” Sara reflects. The absence of family members, a crucial source of emotional support, was especially hard to witness in the hospital setting. “It was tough emotionally to watch them suffer alone, and that affected me.”

The constant fear of infection loomed large for Sara. As a student with less experience in clinical settings, the fear of contracting the virus—and potentially transmitting it to loved ones—was a heavy burden. “I was always worried about getting infected. That fear was a constant presence in my mind,” she admits.

One of the greatest challenges Sara faced was the immense pressure placed on healthcare workers during the pandemic, particularly due to staff shortages and the overwhelming influx of patients. “The high workload was really difficult to manage, especially with the limited resources,” Sara explains. The stress of working under these conditions created further challenges in providing the level of care she aspired to.

But despite the overwhelming pressure, Sara found that collaboration was key to coping with the chaos. “Working with colleagues became more important than ever,” she says. “We had to coordinate with other departments, learn to support each other in new ways, and develop a stronger sense of teamwork.” Even in the face of immense pressure, the sense of solidarity among the healthcare workers was a crucial element that helped them push through

As a student, the steep learning curve was both a challenge and an opportunity. “I gained practical experience much faster than I would have in a normal situation. It was an intense learning environment, but it has made me more prepared for my career,” Sara notes. Yet, she also acknowledges the difficulty of balancing studies with the demands of working on the frontlines. “Combining my studies with clinical practice under such difficult circumstances was hard. There were many times I felt overwhelmed,” she admits.

Reflecting on the experience, Sara believes that better preparedness could have made a significant difference. “If there had been more access to protective equipment and mental health resources, it could have helped a lot. Many of us struggled with stress and burnout,” she says. The lack of mental health support for healthcare workers during the crisis remains a key takeaway from their experience.

A Health Secretary in Medical Biochemistry:

Battling Uncertainty and Exhaustion

For Nora (39), a health secretary working in the medical biochemistry department, the pandemic was equally challenging, albeit from a different perspective. Unlike frontline nurses and doctors, Nora worked behind the scenes in a fast-paced environment that demanded precision and flexibility. "It was extremely difficult," she recalls. "We had very hectic days, and there was always this constant fear of contamination." As part of the team responsible for processing COVID-19 tests, Nora dealt with the logistical and emotional pressures of the pandemic in her way.

The need to wear full protective gear at all times made even simple tasks difficult. “We had to wear special gloves, face masks that were difficult to breathe through, and long gowns. Every time we entered a patient’s room, we had to change our gear. It was physically exhausting,” Nora explains. The department worked tirelessly, processing tests for patients suspected of having COVID-19. Each patient was isolated upon arrival, and Nora was often in contact with individuals who might be infected but weren’t yet confirmed.

“The hardest part was the constant risk of exposure,” Nora says. "We had to work with patients who weren’t always isolated. And when you entered a room, you had to change your protective gear each time, which added to the stress.” The routine of constant disinfection, frequent changes of protective clothing, and the fear of contamination contributed to the mental fatigue that permeated the entire healthcare system.

For her, the emotional toll of the pandemic was deeply felt, particularly when patients passed away. "It was heartbreaking to see so many people die, especially when the vaccines weren’t available at the beginning," she reflects. Witnessing the deaths of patients, often without the support of their families, was one of the most difficult aspects of her role. "I saw many deaths in front of my eyes. It was hard to come to terms with the fact that many patients wouldn’t survive," Nora says solemnly.

In addition to the emotional challenges, practical tasks became increasingly complicated. “One of the greatest challenges was doing blood tests while wearing gloves, which made it harder to find veins,” she recalls. The physical difficulty of performing basic procedures under such conditions added to the stress that healthcare workers were already experiencing.

Despite these challenges, collaboration with other departments and healthcare workers helped to alleviate some of the pressure. "There was good communication between departments. We had clear protocols in place, such as signs on doors and constant access to sanitizers and protective equipment," she says. This collective effort made the impossible seem just a little bit more manageable

The post-pandemic phase

As the world moves into the post-pandemic phase, the nursing student, Sara, the health secretary, Nora, reflect on the lessons that they’ve learned. For Sara, the experience was a stark reminder of the importance of mental health support for healthcare workers. “We need to focus on mental health. It’s something that should have been prioritized,” she stresses. The intense workload, combined with the psychological toll, could have been mitigated with better access to mental health resources

Nora, who worked in medical biochemistry, echoes this sentiment. “There needs to be better preparedness in terms of both equipment and mental health support. We were pushed to our limits, and while we got through it, it was incredibly taxing," she says. "The focus has to be on ensuring that healthcare workers are supported—not just physically, but emotionally—during such a crisis.”

Both also agree that the experience has made them more resilient and better prepared for future challenges. "The pandemic was a wake-up call for the entire healthcare system," Sara reflects. "We learned a lot about collaboration, adaptability, and the importance of taking care of our well-being. We have to be ready for the next crisis, no matter what form it takes."

Future Pandemics: Are We Truly Prepared?

As the world emerges from the COVID-19 pandemic, one key question remains: Are healthcare systems now better prepared for future health threats? Both Sara, a nursing student, and Nora, a health secretary in medical biochemistry, agree that while there have been significant improvements, the pandemic's impact reminds us that no one can truly be “ready” for a crisis of this magnitude.

For Sara, the pandemic underscored the need for flexibility and resilience. “We are definitely more prepared, but I don’t think anyone is ever fully ready for something like this,” she reflects. The emotional toll of the pandemic, despite better planning and equipment, created significant stress and burnout among healthcare workers. Both Sara and Nora highlight the lack of emotional support during such a high-stress period as a crucial gap in the system.

Nora agrees that healthcare systems have made progress, particularly in terms of equipment and training. However, she stresses that the real lesson was in support—both physical and emotional. “We were better prepared than at the start of the pandemic, but healthcare workers need more than just resources; they need emotional support,” Nora says. “Constant exposure to trauma showed us that mental health is as important as physical protection.”

With new health threats, like monkeypox, emerging, Sara and Nora are concerned about whether healthcare workers can continue to rise to the challenge. Sara believes the pandemic strengthened her sense of purpose, making her realize the deeper meaning of her work. “Working through COVID-19 gave me a sense that my job was part of something bigger,” she says.

However, both Sara and Nora emphasize the unsustainable pressure on healthcare workers. “We can’t keep pushing workers to the brink without proper support,” Sara warns. “We need to take care of them, emotionally as well as physically, to ensure a strong, resilient workforce for future crises.”

As the threat of future health emergencies grows, the question remains: will we learn from the mistakes of the past? Both Sara and Nora agree that the lessons from COVID-19 must shape future healthcare policies. “The pandemic showed us where we were lacking,” says Sara. “It’s not just about equipment or resources; it’s about supporting the people who do the hard work every day.”

The experience of healthcare workers during the pandemic serves as both a reminder of the challenges they faced and a call to action for better support systems in the future. By addressing the emotional and psychological needs of healthcare workers, we can hope to build a more resilient healthcare system for whatever challenges lie ahead

Conclusion

The stories of Sara, the nursing student, and Nora, the health secretary, are just two examples of the many experiences healthcare workers had during the COVID-19 pandemic. Their reflections remind us of the emotional and physical toll the pandemic took on those who remained on the front lines. As the world emerges from this crisis, it is clear that the lessons learned will shape the future of healthcare and how we support those who dedicate their lives to keeping others safe.
